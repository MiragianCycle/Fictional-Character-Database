PERSONATA helps writers, novelists, game designers craft compelling fictional characters.

I developed PERSONATA to help me with my own writing work. Before I chained all these processes into this single tool, PERSONATA was a collection of various workflows involving Excel Sheets, Markdown, endless screenshots, to get a single PDF. All of these disparate processes and tools have been chained together in one tool now. It is primarily for my use, but if anyone is interested, feel free to use as you see fit.

How did PERSONATA come about?

Okay story time.

PERSONATA is the result of over a decade of work. Back when I started writing my fiction, I would begin with a list of characters. I would list these characters and use this list as a reference when writing the actual story. But this list had zero uses besides a tool for remembering the names of characters. I needed more.

I flirted with psychological profiling these fictional people. My system was simple:

    1. A fictional person would be moulded by two phenomena: their self esteem, and their social reputation
    2. No person's self esteem and social reputation stays static over their lifetimes.
    3. I could score both phenomena on a scale of 0-5. i.e. A person with 5 as their self-esteem is a gigantic egotist/narcissit, a person with 0 self-esteem is probably in need of serious mental help intervention. Very few people - either in the real world or in fictional worlds - are 0s or 5s. Most people fall into a range between 1-3 their entire lives.
    4. Likewise, a person with a 5 social reputation is probably a saint, and someone with a 0 social reputation is probably a war criminal. Again, very few poeple in life have social reputations of 0 or 5. Most go through 1-3 through the course of their lives.
    5. Finding the difference between Self-Esteem and Social Reputation (i.e. Self-Esteem - Social Reputation) gave me a number. I decided to call this number the Psychological Index.
    6. If a person's self-esteem and social reputation changes over the course of their lives, it stands to reason that this Psychological Index would also change during their lifetime.
    7. If I were to track a fictional person's Self-Esteem and Social Reputations over the course of my narrative - and let's assume we are measuring narrative time as Acts [i.e. Act 1, Act 2, and Act 3] - we arrive at three Psychological Indicies over the same narrative time.
    8. Plot Psychological Index as a line graph over time (i.e. Narrative Time measured in acts), you get a literal character arc.

I moved this system from pen and paper to Excel sheet, since I needed to create tables and charts to see the system at work. Here is an early screenshot of the system in an Excel Worksheet.

PERSONATA is the appification (is that a word?) of this system so I don't have to spend time on Excel and Charts, instead of writing.

1. Adding Characters

Start by adding your characters to the database. On the home page, you'll find a form to enter a character's name and description. This is your chance to give a brief overview of who they are.

2. Character Arcs

Once you've added a character, you can define their arc. This is broken down into three acts, mirroring the typical three-act structure of many stories. For each act, you'll enter:

  • Self-Esteem: How the character views themselves (scale of 0-5)
  • Social Reputation: How others view the character (scale of 0-5)

The tool will calculate a Psychological Index for each act, which is the difference between Self-Esteem and Social Reputation. This can help you track your character's internal conflicts and growth.

3. Relationships

You can also define relationships between characters. This includes the type of relationship and its intensity on a scale of 1-10. This feature can help you keep track of complex character dynamics in your story.

4. Comparing Characters

Use the Compare Characters feature to see how different characters' arcs progress side by side. This can be particularly useful for ensemble casts or for comparing protagonists and antagonists.

Remember, this tool is here to assist your creative process, not to constrain it. Use it in whatever way best supports your writing. Happy storytelling!
